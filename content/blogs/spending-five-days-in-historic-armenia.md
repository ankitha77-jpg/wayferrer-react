On this blog post, we talk about the less frequently visited Armenia (unless you live in Dubai and hop on a flight to go here for sport). After all, a short 3 hour flight from Dubai will take you to this small country in the Caucasus region.

![armenia visa for indians](/blog-images/spending-five-days-in-historic-armenia-1.jpg)

Armenia is the second country I have visited in this region that is nestled in the crossroads between Eastern Europe and Western Asia.

![armenia visa for indians](/blog-images/spending-five-days-in-historic-armenia-3.jpg)

It is also surprisingly historic and it is believed that they were one of the first countries in the world to adopt Christianity as their state religion. Be prepared for a whole lot of beautiful **Armenia monasteries** scattered around the region amongst dense national park forests and snow-capped alps.

You can no longer get a visa on arrival. You will need a visa. You can apply for this yourself. **Armenia visa for Indians** requires applying in advance. Heads up - sometimes, you could be rejected if you have an Indian passport. The agency I went with applied for me. You can directly apply for it [here](https://evisa.mfa.am/) too.

NOTE: Due to the conflicts in the region, if by any chance you have been to Azerbaijan in the recent past, expect to be questioned about this at the immigration counter. Keep your responses short and be clear about the reasons for your trip there. if you went for tourist reasons, explain that clearly and be ready to produce a detailed set of documents for your trip to Armenia from **Armenia itinerary**, hotel reservations, and return ticket.

Expect your typical developing country culture. Most certainly expect a few leers from strange men on the street if you decide to walk outdoors in a fancy dress in the city. Go with your guide/driver and be mindful of your surroundings — especially if you’re considering **Armenia **

April, May, June and October, November is best due to the slightly cooler weather. The **best time to visit Armenia** really depends on whether you prefer snowy landscapes or mild spring hikes.

Everyone is helpful and fluent in English.

Travel Insurance is highly recommended. USD accepted at all resorts. Carry local currency for small purchases.

For this trip with an amazing local guide. The owner, set us up wonderfully. No hassles and extremely hands-on. They have a location in Yerevan, Armenia as well as Dubai. Smooth transactions and she was happy to accommodate any requests at a marginal cost.

This helped us cover all the highlights quickly as part of our **Armenia travel guide** experience.

Food is ridiculously cheap, so is transportation and hotel. Including hotel, flights and transportation, the trip should cost no more than $650-800, depending on where you stay and time/length of travel.

The food was great in the city. There were a lot of wonderful upscale restaurants just around [Ramada](https://www.stay22.com/allez/roam?aid=wayfarerfootprints&campaign=ankitha77wixsite-spendingfivedaysinhistoricarmenia&product=lma&habl=false&isinc=true&sid22=vD3M8GEzbfrFwZw0&adStack=google&hasCTZ=false&hasTP=false&conmeth=normal&plng=en&pageCategory=travel&source=direct&medium=hyperlink&address=Yerevan%2C+Armenia&hotelname=Ramada&search=Ramada&category=accommodation&version=surface-the-opportunities%7C7%7C1.2.0&ref22=https%3A%2F%2Fankitha77.wixsite.com%2Fwayfarerfootprints%2Fpost%2Fspending-five-days-in-historic-armenia). Some so fancy that we needed a reservation on a weekday. Ha!

The food on the road was a miss, as always. Nothing special. On our last night though, we went to a beautiful restaurant called Zaitoon in downtown Yerevan. The ambiance and service was upbeat accompanied by some delicious vegan food. Definitely give it a try.

Top tip: we found this joint called Fresh House. They have some epic milkshakes, coffee and hot chocolate. It's just a small shack in the middle of the city.

Today we explored the Southern region of the country. Starting with Noravank Monastery. Just a short drive from Yerevan, it was so busy even at 11 a.m. in the morning. Expect crowds no matter when you go.

This takes you right up to the Tatev Monastery, built in the 9th century. It also happens to be one of the most far-flung ones in the country.. which means most people don’t make it here.
